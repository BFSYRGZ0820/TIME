To achieve A-S balance, existing CTTA strategies mainly
fall into (Wang et al., 2025): self-regularization (SR) and
teacher-student consistency regularization (CR). The SR
(Niu et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2024b) introduces regularization to constrain the model updates, preventing extremely
far away from the source domain. Although this strategy
preserves source-domain knowledge and partially improves
stability in CTTA, the constraint restricts the degree of adaptation to the test domain, thereby constraining the adaptability. The CR (Wang et al., 2022; Rafi et al., 2025) uses a
student that adapts to the current domain to improve adaptability, while a teacher aggregates past domain knowledge
to provide stable guidance, improving stability. However,
since the teacher is continuously updated by the student,
the teacher tends to be increasingly influenced by recent domains. As domain shifts accumulate, the teacher gradually
forgets early domains, limiting long-term stable guidance
in CTTA. Both strategies are limited by the model architecture, as it encodes knowledge from all domains into a
single global set of parameters, which is typically called
Dense architectures1 . As a result, adapting the dense models
to a new domain inevitably covers parameters that encode
knowledge from previous domains, making it challenging
for dense models to achieve A-S balance in CTTA.

on a router to allocate samples to different experts, allowing
each expert to focus on different domain samples and enabling the model to adapt to the current domain by updating
only a few experts without affecting global knowledge. At
the same time, experts who retain knowledge from previous

However, despite the above advantages, integrating MoE
architectures with CTTA is underexplored and faces challenges as follows: (1) The effectiveness of the router relies
on knowledge learned from the source domains during training. When it encounters samples from unseen domains that
continuously shifting during testing, the router may allocate them to incorrect experts, leading to routing errors. (2)
MoE routing relies on token-level representations, whereas
CTTA exhibits continuous domain-level shifts across samples (i.e., shifts that are encoded in the evolving distribution
of sample-level statistics). This level mismatch prevents
the router from capturing evolving domain characteristics,
further leading to routing errors. Fig. 1b shows the routing
errors of the original MoE router, where it tends to allocate
samples to experts randomly. As a result, routing errors
break the A-S balance originally achievable by the MoE.
To mitigate routing errors in CTTA, we argue that it is necessary to explore a CTTA method under the MoE architecture that computes the effective samples‚Äìexperts allocation
scheme at test time based on the current sample and expert
parameters, achieving a better A-S balance during CTTA.
In this paper, we propose a test-time mixture-of-experts routing (TiME) framework via asymmetric co-optimal transport
(As-COOT) for CTTA. We use co-optimal transport (COOT)
to model the test-time sample‚Äìexpert allocation problem.

To ensure the effectiveness of the allocation, we align the
semantic spaces of samples and experts. To address the
limitations of COOT in CTTA with MoE models, we propose As-COOT, which meets the requirements of discarding
high-cost noisy samples and constraining expert load balance. Specifically, (1) to enable MoE routing in CTTA, we
model it as a test-time sample-expert allocation problem
via COOT, which optimizes the minimum-cost allocation
scheme from the sample‚Äìexpert allocation cost, guiding
online routing. (2) For the allocation scheme to be semantically meaningful, the sample‚Äìexpert allocation cost needs
to reflect the functional compatibility between samples and
experts. To meet this, we propose a sample‚Äìexpert semantic
space alignment module that aligns their representations via
bidirectional contrastive learning, so that a distance-based
metric can serve as a proxy for the allocation cost, enabling
effective cost computation. (3) To mitigate noise effects
and achieve balanced expert load, thereby better addressing
CTTA, we propose the As-COOT that applies relaxation to
the sample-side mass constraint to discard high-cost noisy
samples, while imposing expert-side constraints to enforce
expert load balance, and we solve As-COOT using block
coordinate descent with entropy regularization, which yields
a strictly convex objective and allows efficient parallel optimization via the Fenchel‚ÄìRockafellar dual and Sinkhorn
scaling. TiME outperforms all baselines on the CTTA benchmark, including text understanding and medical reasoning.
Our contributions are: 1) Our analysis substantiates that
MoE architecture models are more effective than dense models for CTTA. 2) We model and formalize the test-time MoE
routing problem via COOT, and explore the limitations of
COOT under CTTA. 3) We propose TiME, a CTTA method
for MoE models that effectively mitigates test-time routing errors by using our proposed As-COOT, which relaxes
the sample side and constrains the expert side to discard
high-cost noise and balance expert loads during CTTA. 4)
Experiments show that TiME achieves SOTA performance
on the CTTA benchmarks across multiple MoE models.

Routing in Mixture-of-Experts LLMs. MoE routing allocates input tokens to a subset of experts. It falls into two
types (Cai et al., 2025): 1) Training-based routing adopts a
parameterized router to map token representations to expert
weights. Qwen1.5-MoE (Team, 2024) stabilizes training via
load balancing and regularization. DeepSeek-V2 (Liu et al.,
2024a) improves expert specialization by fine-grained segmentation. R2-T2 (Li et al., 2025) re-routes guided by reference samples. 2) Training-free routing uses algorithmic allocation to route the expert. Hash Layer (Roller et al., 2021)
uses a random router by hashing. THOR (Zuo et al., 2021b)
randomly routes experts with consistency. Ban&Pick (Chen
et al., 2025b) prunes redundant activations. Rewiring (Su
et al., 2025) self-supervised via router logit. Training-based
routing is tightly coupled to the training domain and thus
fails during testing due to domain shift. Training-free routing lacks parameter semantic modeling. We aim to design
an online routing to address both limitations.

3. Method
Overview. To address the routing errors of MoE models
in CTTA, we propose TiME (as shown in Fig. 2). (1) The
test-time MoE routing framework (¬ß 3.1) remodels testtime MoE routing in CTTA as a test-time sample‚Äìexpert
allocation problem. which is instantiated by optimizing a
sample‚Äìexpert allocation scheme via COOT. (2) The semantic space alignment (SSA) module (¬ß 3.2) aligns the
semantic spaces of sample and expert distributions by pushing functionally compatible sample-expert pairs closer via
bidirectional contrastive learning, enabling a distance-based
sample‚Äìexpert allocation cost. (3) The As-COOT (¬ß 3.3) is
designed to address the limitations of COOT under CTTA
in MoE models by relaxing sample-side constraints and enforcing expert-side constraints, thereby guaranteeing noise
discard and balancing expert load. TiME‚Äôs pipeline operates
at test time, as follows: we first model the test-time MoE
routing problem via COOT (¬ß 3.1). Then, given the current
domain sample and expert distributions, TiME aligns their
semantic spaces via SSA (¬ß 3.2). Based on the aligned distributions, As-COOT computes the test-time sample‚Äìexpert
allocation scheme to calibrate the original MoE routing. Finally, we adapt the model with the calibrated experts (¬ß 3.3).

2) Backpropagation-based methods update the model by
optimizing. Tent (Wang et al., 2021) adapts by minimizing
entropy. CoTTA (Wang et al., 2022) uses a teacher-student
framework and data augmentation. SAR (Niu et al., 2023)
uses sharpness-aware minimization (SAM). SoTTA (Gong
et al., 2023) buffers class-balanced samples and SAM. DeYo
(Lee et al., 2024b) adapts images using consistency under

To address the test-time MoE routing (i.e., allocation) problem, CO-Optimal Transport (COOT) (Titouan et al., 2020)
provides a paradigm for allocation. We propose a testtime MoE routing (TTR) framework that determines sample‚Äìexpert allocation schemes at test time based on the current sample and expert distributions, mitigating the original
MoE routing errors in CTTA. In MoE, routing schemes are
produced by a pretrained router. However, under CTTA, the
router, trained on the source domain, may not encounter samples from shifting domains (Pt Ã∏= Pt‚àí1 ), and may therefore

domains rather than relying on the fixed pretrained router.
Accordingly, we remodel test-time MoE routing in CTTA
as a test-time sample-expert allocation problem.

3.2. Semantic Space Alignment for Allocation Cost
To ensure the effectiveness of COOT-based sample-expert
allocation, the allocation cost should faithfully reflect the
sample-expert functional compatibility. We therefore introduce a sample-expert semantic space alignment (SSA)
module, which aligns the semantic spaces of sample and
expert via bidirectional contrastive learning. It enables the
use of a distance-based metric to model sample‚Äìexpert functional compatibility as allocation cost. SSA is crucial, as
the effectiveness of COOT for test-time MoE routing critically depends on the quality and interpretability of the
allocation cost L(Xto , Eto ) (Eq. 2). However, Xto and Eto lie
in heterogeneous spaces2 , rendering the L(Xto , Eto ), computed from original representations, physically meaningless.
Our SSA addresses this issue by aligning sample-expert
semantic spaces through modeling sample-expert functional
compatibility, enabling a distance metric as a proxy for the
sample-expert allocation cost. Specifically, we train a sample encoder fs (¬∑) and an expert encoder fe (¬∑) to minimize
the distance between a sample representation fs (x) and
its compatible expert representation fe (e) via bidirectional
contrastive learning, achieving allocation costs measured
by distance metric (lower distance, smaller cost). We train
SSA in an offline preprocessing stage, and adopt Euclidean
distance d(x, e) = ‚à•fs (x)‚àífe (e)‚à•2 as the distance metric.

We propose TiME, a novel test-time MoE routing method
for CTTA. We model a test-time sample‚Äìexpert routing problem via COOT, which enables online allocation between
current domain samples and MoE experts during CTTA. To
ensure effective routing, we align sample and expert distributions in an aligned semantic space, modeling functional
compatibility as a distance-based allocation cost. Above all,
we propose As-COOT to address the limitations of COOT
in CTTA with MoE models by relaxing sample-side constraints and enforcing expert-side constraints, thereby ensuring effective noise discarding and expert load balancing.
Experiments show that TiME achieves SOTA performance.

Figure 5. Performance comparison of MoE (Qwen1.5-MoE-A2.7B) and Dense (Qwen1.5-7B and Qwen1.5-4B) architectures when used
as base models under various CTTA methods on Text Understanding task stream. Source denotes zero-shot predictions without adaptation.

To empirically validate the structural effectiveness and suitability of MoE over Dense architectures as base models for CTTA,
we conducted a comparative experiment using Qwen1.5-MoE-A2.7B against two dense models: Qwen1.5-4B, which has a
comparable number of active parameters, and Qwen1.5-7B, which exhibits stronger zero-shot performance. The comparison
is conducted across seven mainstream CTTA methods. As shown in Fig. 5, the Dense-7B model achieves the highest
zero-shot performance (0.4544), which is expected given its larger parameter scale. However, this advantage does not persist
under continuous adaptation. Specifically, under methods like Tent and CoTTA, the performance of the Dense-7B sharply
degrades to 0.3543 and 0.3579, respectively, falling below the MoE baseline. In contrast, Qwen1.5-MoE-A2.7B shows
substantially better robustness during adaptation. Under Tent, it achieves 0.3887, clearly outperforming both Dense-4B
(0.3223) and Dense-7B (0.3543). Moreover, when combined with CTTA-T, the MoE model reaches a peak performance of
0.4548, which even exceeds the original zero-shot performance of the much larger Dense-7B model. Although one might
expect Dense-7B to maintain its superiority during adaptation, the results reveal the opposite trend. Dense architectures,
when adopted as CTTA base models, are highly vulnerable to continuous adaptation in CTTA settings, whereas the MoE
architecture remains significantly more stable.
These results indicate that the MoE architecture is more suitable as a base model for CTTA. Although the Dense-7B
model starts with strong source (zero-shot) performance, it experiences severe performance degradation during continuous
adaptation in CTTA. In contrast, the MoE model consistently outperforms both Dense-4B and Dense-7B under most CTTA
methods. This advantage comes from the sparse expert structure of MoE. During adaptation, only a subset of experts needs
to update to fit the current domain, which improves adaptability. Meanwhile, the remaining experts stay largely isolated,
helping preserve global knowledge and maintain overall stability.
Nevertheless, it is important to note that even when adopting a MoE architecture that is more suitable as a CTTA base
model, existing CTTA methods still struggle to fully exploit its potential. As shown in Fig. 5, under most CTTA methods,
the adapted performance of the MoE model remains comparable to or even below its zero-shot performance, indicating
limited adaptation gains. This observation suggests that the current CTTA algorithms are not sufficiently effective when
applied to MoE-based models, and in many cases fail to surpass simple zero-shot inference without adaptation. Therefore,
merely replacing the base model with a more suitable MoE architecture is not enough. It is necessary to develop a more
effective CTTA method that is specifically designed to align with the characteristics of MoE architectures, in order to unlock
their full potential under CTTA.

The objective in Eq. 48 is convex with respect to œÄ s , as it consists of a linear cost term, a convex KL divergence over the

5. Summary
We have rigorously established the following properties for As-COOT. 1) Well-Posedness: The problem is defined on a
compact set with a lower semi-continuous objective, guaranteeing the existence of an optimal solution (Theorem 1). 2)
Tractability: The non-convex joint problem decomposes into strictly convex sub-problems. Through Fenchel duality, we
proved that the sub-problems admit a unique solution expressible via efficient generalized Sinkhorn scalings (Theorem 2). 3)
Algorithmic Convergence: The Block Coordinate Descent strategy is theoretically guaranteed to monotonically decrease the
objective and converge to a stationary point (Theorem 3).
This theoretical foundation confirms that As-COOT is not merely a heuristic, but a mathematically sound optimization
framework for asymmetric distribution alignment.

F. Analysis of Noisy Samples
F.1. Noisy Samples in CTTA: Definition and Rationale
In CTTA, models are exposed to an unlabeled and continuously evolving test stream, where domain shifts accumulate
over time. Unlike standard i.i.d. test-time inference, CTTA inevitably encounters a substantial portion of samples that are
unreliable for adaptation. Prior CTTA studies have observed this phenomenon under different terms, such as low-confidence
samples (Wang et al., 2023), hard samples (Zuo et al., 2021a), or unreliable pseudo-labeled (Wang et al., 2022) instances,
and have shown that adapting on all test samples often leads to error accumulation and performance degradation.
In this work, we define noise in CTTA explicitly as samples with high allocation cost. From a functional and allocationcentric perspective, a test sample is regarded as noisy if it cannot be reliably assigned to any expert under the current model
state. Such samples typically arise during domain transitions, at distribution boundaries, or in regions where the evolving
test distribution is insufficiently covered by the existing expert functionalities. Importantly, they are not necessarily out-ofdistribution or permanently uninformative; rather, they are temporarily incompatible with the current expert ensemble. After

As-COOT addresses this issue by relaxing the sample-side marginal constraint via a KL divergence penalty, allowing

when they are not yet compatible with the expert ensemble. This design choice is validated by our ablation studies (as

performance degradation, demonstrating that explicitly accounting for noisy, high-cost samples is critical for stable and
effective CTTA. Together, these analyses justify our treatment of high allocation cost samples as noise and highlight the
necessity of As-COOT in CTTA.

H.1. Implementation Details
We conduct experiments on Qwen1.5-MoE-A2.7B-Chat (Team, 2024) and DeepSeek-V2-Lite (Liu et al., 2024a). Qwen1.5MoE-A2.7B-Chat is a Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) model with 14.3B total parameters and 2.7B activated parameters at
runtime; it uses 24 MoE layers, each with 60 experts and a shared expert per MoE layer. DeepSeek-V2-Lite is a 15.7Bparameter MoE model with 2.4B activated parameters, comprising 1 MLP layer and 26 MoE layers, where each MoE layer
is composed of 64 routed experts and 2 shared experts. We use all-MiniLM-L6-v2 as the sample encoder and a two-layer
MLP with GELU activation and ‚Ñì2 normalization as the expert encoder, where the expert encoder takes as input the flattened
LoRA adapter parameters extracted from each expert at a given layer (encoders are mentioned in Sec. 3.2). Both encoders
are trained with temperature œÑ = 0.07. We optimize the sample encoder and the expert encoder jointly using the Adam
optimizer (Kinga et al., 2015). LoRA adapters with rank r = 16 and scaling factor Œ± = 16 are injected into all expert MLP
modules, and their outputs are added to the original expert activations. To ensure fair comparison and reduce computational
overhead, all methods uniformly inject LoRA adapters when using MoE-based models. All experiments are performed on a
single NVIDIA A800 GPU. We use the AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017) with a batch size of 8 for Qwen
and 4 for DeepSeek, and a learning rate of 1e-5. For expert routing, we activate the top-2 experts per MoE layer. Unless
otherwise specified, the hyperparameters are set to Œ± = 5.0, Œª = 0.1, and p = 0.01 across all experiments. By default, our
TiME is enabled at the first MoE layer of the model (layer 0 for Qwen and layer 1 for DeepSeek), and is implemented using
only a single MoE layer. The performance and analysis of TiME across different MoE layers are provided in the App. N.5.

H.5. Baselines
We compare our TiME with mainstream CTTA methods. Tent (Wang et al., 2021) updates the BN parameters via minimizing
the prediction entropy. CoTTA (Wang et al., 2022) combines a teacher-student framework and data augmentation. SAR
(Niu et al., 2023) uses sharpness-aware minimization to find a flatter loss space. SoTTA (Gong et al., 2023) utilizes a
sharpness-aware optimizer to improve the reliability of model updates and facilitate adaptation to highly challenging
environments. DSS (Wang et al., 2024b) uses a teacher-student framework and mitigates noise through positive-negative
learning. REM (Han et al., 2025) enforces difficulty-aware adaptation via ranking masked consistency. CTTA-T (Liu
et al., 2025) uses a teacher-student framework to perceive domain shift and refine-then-filter noisy sample via prediction
consistency.
We compare our TiME with mainstream online MoE re-routing methods. THOR (Zuo et al., 2021b) randomly allocates
experts to each input with consistency. Rewiring (Su et al., 2025) leverages self-supervision via lightweight router logit
updates to rewire expert selection during testing. R2-T2 (Li et al., 2025) leverages optimization guided by reference samples,
performing expert re-routing without additional training during testing.

I. Evaluation of Time and Memory Efficiency
Since CTTA is in an online setting, both adaptation time and memory are critical factors. As shown in Tab. 4, TiME
introduces acceptable, marginal time and memory overhead compared with the source model and Tent, while remaining
substantially more efficient than other baselines. This efficiency stems from As-COOT, the core theoretical foundation
of TiME, whose optimization can be efficiently solved via GPU parallelism, enabling fast optimization of the adaptation
process (see complexity analysis in App. G). TiME leverages an As-COOT‚Äìdriven discarding strategy to adapt on a small
set of high-quality samples, reducing memory consumption. In contrast, teacher‚Äìstudent‚Äìbased methods such as CoTTA,
DSS, and CTTA-T require maintaining multiple models, leading to higher time and memory. SAR, SoTTA, and REM rely
on complex parameter adaptation or sample processing strategies, which increase both time and memory.

J. Evaluation of Time and Memory Efficiency of Each Module
We conduct a module-wise evaluation of time and memory efficiency, as shown in Tab. 5, to analyze the overhead introduced
by each module in TiME.
Using expert output entropy (EOE) in TiME results in higher memory usage and time compared to original MoE routing
(OR). This is because EOE requires forwarding each sample through all experts and computing per-expert output entropy,
which introduces additional overhead. In contrast, OR directly leverages the pretrained router to identify compatible experts,
avoiding exhaustive expert evaluation and leading to a more efficient SSA implementation. Consequently, OR achieves a
better efficiency‚Äìperformance trade-off and is adopted as the default strategy.
Replacing As-COOT with COOT leads to higher memory consumption. This is mainly attributed to the sample-side
relaxation in As-COOT. By allowing the model to discard high-cost noisy samples, As-COOT effectively reduces the
number of samples participating in adaptation, resulting in lower memory. In contrast, COOT enforces stricter mass
conservation, forcing all samples to remain involved in routing and allocation, which increases memory. Notably, As-COOT

L. Comparison with Methods of Online MoE Re-Routing Setting
To further assess the generality and positioning of our TiME in the CTTA setting, we additionally compare TiME with
several representative online MoE re-routing baselines.
Task Definition: Online MoE re-routing aims to address the performance degradation caused by suboptimal routing
decisions when pretrained MoE routers encounter out-of-domain test samples. Since routing functions are learned on
training domains, they may fail to generalize to domain shifts at test time, leading to inefficient expert selection. To this end,
online MoE re-routing optimizes expert selection during testing by adapting routing behaviors based on test-time signals,
while keeping the base model parameters frozen.
We note that these methods are not designed for CTTA and do not explicitly account for online domain shifts in evolving
test streams, which are central to CTTA. Instead, they primarily focus on improving MoE inference by optimizing routing
decisions at test time under a fixed task domain. Nevertheless, since these methods also aim to mitigate suboptimal expert
selection at test time without retraining model parameters, they provide a meaningful point of comparison. Including them
allows us to better contextualize our method within the broader landscape of test-time MoE routing techniques and to
demonstrate the advantages of CTTA-aware routing under online and non-stationary scenarios.

As shown in Tab. 7, our TiME consistently achieves the best average performance, showing its effectiveness in addressing
test-time routing errors under CTTA. Among the compared baselines, THOR performs the worst and even underperforms
the zero-shot source model on most tasks. This is expected, as THOR relies on random expert allocation with only routing

reliance on noisy contextual signals as self-supervision during unlabeled testing, as well as the lack of explicit expert load
balancing, which leads to expert collapse and degrades generalization. R2-T2 is the strongest baseline among online MoE
re-routing methods, achieving performance close to TiME. Its effectiveness mainly stems from retrieval-based guidance
using labeled training samples for test-time re-routing. However, this reliance on labeled source-domain samples limits its
practicality under continuous domain shifts. In contrast, TiME strictly operates in the CTTA setting without any labeled
test-time samples, better reflecting real-world deployment scenarios with unlabeled and evolving test streams.
Overall, this comparison shows that while online MoE re-routing methods are effective within their own problem settings,
where test-time routing is optimized for fixed or task-specific domains, their assumptions differ fundamentally from those of
CTTA. In contrast, CTTA explicitly models realistic deployment conditions, characterized by evolving test domains and
the absence of labeled supervision. By addressing this more practical and challenging setting, TiME is better aligned with
real-world scenarios that require continuous, unsupervised adaptation to non-stationary testing domain streams.

M. Why Semantic Space Alignment Learned Offline Remains Valid in CTTA
Semantic Space Alignment module (SSA, ¬ß 3.2), which is learned in an offline preprocessing stage with fixed encoders,
remains valid throughout the continual test-time adaptation (CTTA) process, although expert parameters evolve over time.
and that static alignment is not only sufficient but also desirable in CTTA, and that updating SSA online may instead
introduce instability and semantic drift.
First, SSA aligns sample‚Äìexpert functional compatibility rather than instantaneous parameter values. The alignment
captures the relative suitability between samples and experts at the level of functional roles and specialization. Although
expert parameters are updated during CTTA, these updates refine experts locally to better fit evolving domains and do not
fundamentally alter their functional semantics. Therefore, the global compatibility structure learned by SSA remains stable.
Second, expert adaptation in CTTA is smooth and constrained. Updates are performed with small optimization steps and
regularization (e.g., entropy minimization and random restoration), which prevents abrupt semantic shifts or role collapse.
As a result, expert evolution preserves the semantic geometry in which SSA operates, ensuring that distance-based allocation
costs remain meaningful over time.
Third, keeping SSA fixed is a deliberate design choice. Allowing SSA to adapt online with unlabeled and continuously
shifting test data would couple alignment learning with noisy updates, potentially causing semantic drift and unstable
allocation costs. A static SSA instead serves as a stable semantic anchor, enabling consistent comparison between evolving
experts and current-domain samples.

O. Full Version of Related Work
1837
1838 O.1. Routing in Mixture-of-Experts LLMs
1839
1840 MoE routing allocates input tokens to a subset of experts. It falls into two types (Cai et al., 2025): 1) Training-based
1841 routing adopts a parameterized router to map token representations to expert weights. MoE-Multi (Shazeer et al., 2017)
1842 learns routing decisions jointly with experts via backpropagation. GShard (Lepikhin et al., 2020) trains routing end-to-end
1843 with experts while enforcing load balancing. Qwen1.5-MoE (Team, 2024) stabilizes MoE training through load balancing
1844 and entropy regularization. DeepSeek-V2 (Liu et al., 2024a) improves expert specialization by fine-grained segmentation.
1845 ReMoE (Wang et al., 2024c) proposes a differentiable ReLU-based router. Lory (Zhong et al., 2024) introduces a causal
1846 differentiable routing that retains sequence causality. MoR (Zhang et al., 2025) enhances robustness by multiple sub-routers.
1847 Rewiring (Su et al., 2025) leverages data-free test-time self-supervision via lightweight router logit updates to dynamically
1848 rewire MoE expert selection on-the-fly. 2) Training-free routing uses algorithmic allocation to route the expert. Hash Layer
1849 (Roller et al., 2021) uses a random router by hashing the token. THOR (Zuo et al., 2021b) randomly allocates experts to
1850 each input with consistency. DEMix (Gururangan et al., 2022) allocates experts to pretraining domains and blends outputs
1851 at inference. PanGu-Œ£ (Ren et al., 2023) routes tokens to the expert group and randomly selects one. Symbolic-MoE
1852 (Chen et al., 2025a) routes queries to experts via inferred skill names in natural language, enabling gradient-free, adaptive
1853 reasoning. Ban&Pick (Chen et al., 2025b) reinforces high-impact experts and prunes redundant activations. R2-T2 (Li et al.,
1854 2025) leverages test-time local optimization guided by reference samples, performing dynamic expert re-routing without
1855 additional training. Training-based routing is tightly coupled to the training domain and thus fails under domain shift during
1856 testing. Training-free routing lacks semantic modeling. We aim to design an online routing to address both limitations.
1857
1858 O.2. Continual Test-Time Adaptation
1859 CTTA is a testing process that mitigates continual domain shift (Wang et al., 2022). It falls into: 1) Non-backpropagation1860 based methods update inputs or outputs during testing. LAME (Boudiaf et al., 2022) adjusts probabilities with Laplace
1861 maximum likelihood. FOA (Niu et al., 2024) uses covariance adaptation to learn input prompts. 2) Backpropagation-based
1862 methods update the model by optimizing using test samples. Tent (Wang et al., 2021) adapts during testing by minimizing
1863 entropy in the BN layer. EATA (Niu et al., 2022) minimizes the samples with low entropy. OIL (Ye et al., 2022) uses a
1864 teacher-student framework and causal inference to improve model adaptability. CoTTA (Wang et al., 2022) uses a teacher1865 student framework and data augmentation. VIDA (Liu et al., 2023) enhances CoTTA by adding learnable lower-order and
1866 higher-order parameters. SAR (Niu et al., 2023) conducts an entropy filter and sharpness-aware entropy minimization.
1867 SoTTA (Gong et al., 2023) buffers class-balanced samples and sharpens entropy minimization. BECoTT (Lee et al., 2024a)
1868 leverages mixture-of-domain low-rank experts with input-dependent domain-adaptive sparse routing. DeYo (Lee et al.,
